If you have been playing Wordle for more than a few weeks, you have probably noticed something: the green-yellow-gray loop is satisfying, but it is also short. Two or three minutes and the day's puzzle is done. Many players end up looking for **complementary daily puzzles** to round out a 10 to 15 minute word-game routine.

The trick is choosing puzzles that **pull on different word skills** so you do not burn out on a single style. Below is a stack that works well, ordered roughly by mental load.

## A balanced daily stack

### 1. Wordle (or a longer variant)

Start with the deliberate, deductive part of your routine. Wordle's 5-letter format is the default, but if you have been playing for a while, try moving to [6-letter](/6-letters) or [7-letter](/7-letters) once a week. The longer formats reward suffix and prefix recognition, which is a different muscle than the consonant-coverage strategy that wins at 5 letters.

### 2. A mini crossword

After Wordle, mini crosswords are a natural second course. They use **trivia and clue-parsing** instead of letter elimination, so they engage a different part of word memory. Most mini crosswords are 5×5 and take about 90 seconds — short enough that they feel like dessert, not a second meal.

### 3. A cryptic clue

This is where things get interesting. Cryptic crosswords are a different beast — each clue is two clues in one: a straight definition, plus a wordplay puzzle that produces the same answer. Most beginners bounce off the full grid, but **a single cryptic clue per day** is the right dose to start.

A site we like for this is [Minute Cryptic](https://minutecryptic.org/) — they post one cryptic clue every day with an explanation. It is genuinely the lowest-friction way to learn cryptic wordplay, because you only have to parse one clue at a time. After a month of daily clues you will start solving cryptics that used to look like nonsense.

### 4. Connections-style grouping

Round out with a category-grouping puzzle. These reward **lateral thinking** — spotting what four words have in common — which is the opposite of Wordle's narrow vertical search. Five minutes here resets your brain before you go back to the world.

## Why this works

Each of the four puzzles above asks for a different kind of word skill:

| Puzzle | Primary skill |
|---|---|
| Wordle | Letter elimination, frequency analysis |
| Mini crossword | Definition recall, short-clue parsing |
| Cryptic clue | Wordplay decoding (anagrams, hidden words, double meaning) |
| Grouping puzzle | Categorical / lateral thinking |

If you only play one type all day, you fatigue that one circuit. Rotating between types is how serious puzzle solvers keep their accuracy high without losing the fun.

## How long should the routine take

Aim for **10 to 15 minutes total**. If your stack is creeping past 30 minutes, you are not enjoying it any more — you are grinding. Cut something. The point of a daily puzzle routine is to start the day with a small win, not to consume your morning.

## Building up to cryptics

Cryptic crosswords have a reputation for being impenetrable, and it is partly true: the language used to signal wordplay (indicators for anagrams, reversals, hidden words, and so on) is a learned vocabulary. But it is a **finite** vocabulary. There are maybe 30 common indicator patterns. Once you internalize them, cryptics become solvable.

The daily one-clue format is genuinely the best on-ramp. Try it for a month — one clue a day, read the explanation when you fail — and you will be surprised how fast cryptic logic clicks.

## A note on streaks across puzzles

It is tempting to chase streaks on every puzzle you add. Resist. Streak anxiety is what turns a fun routine into a chore. Treat any one puzzle as the "anchor" of your streak — Wordle is the obvious choice — and let the others be optional. Some days you will skip the cryptic. That is fine. The routine is for you, not the other way around.
