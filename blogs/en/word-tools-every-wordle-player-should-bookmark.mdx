Most Wordle players are quietly word nerds. We notice etymologies on signs, mentally rearrange license plates, and keep a running tally of "huh, that is a great five-letter word" moments throughout the week. Over time you end up wanting a small set of **word utilities** to support that habit — for writing, for tracking your vocabulary, or just for satisfying random word curiosity.

Below is a short, opinionated list of tools that are actually useful, not the usual SEO-stuffed "top 50 word tools" lists.

## Anagram solvers

Anagram solvers are the most useful single category. They are not just for cheating at Scrabble — used the right way, they teach you which letter combinations actually produce real words, which directly improves your Wordle endgame. When you have `_OUND` left to fill, knowing the consonants that produce real words (B, F, H, M, P, R, S, W) is faster than guessing.

Pick any anagram tool that lets you specify a letter pattern. Use it sparingly, **after** you have attempted the puzzle, not during.

## Word counters

Word counters seem boring until you start writing daily. Whether you are journaling, blogging, posting on social, or writing essays, knowing word count without opening a full word processor is genuinely useful. The good ones also count characters with and without spaces, sentences, and reading time.

For Spanish-speaking readers in particular, [Contador de Palabras](https://contador-de-palabras.com/) is a solid free word counter — it handles Spanish-specific text well (including accented characters, which a lot of generic English counters mishandle) and runs entirely in the browser with no signup. Useful if you write in Spanish or are learning the language.

For English text, any built-in word count in Google Docs or Notion does the job equally well.

## Vocabulary trackers

This category is underrated. If you read a lot, the only way to actually grow your vocabulary is to **revisit new words** — not just look them up once and move on. A simple spaced-repetition app (Anki is the canonical choice) with a "new word I encountered today" deck is the best version of this. Five new entries a day, reviewed for two minutes, will add hundreds of words to your active vocabulary in a year.

Active vocabulary helps Wordle more than people expect. The puzzle's answer list skews toward common-but-not-trivial words, and the more words you know in the 4000–10000 frequency band, the more often you will spot the answer before you have green letters.

## Letter frequency tools

There are free sites that show letter frequency by position in English. The most useful insight: **E** and **S** are the most common end-of-word letters, and **T**, **S**, and **A** dominate the start position. Most Wordle players know this intuitively, but seeing the actual percentages will sharpen your opener choice. If you have never measured your starting word's coverage, do it once and you will probably switch.

## Rhyming dictionaries

Not a Wordle tool, but adjacent. If you write at all — poetry, song lyrics, even just clever taglines — a good rhyming dictionary is a delight to have bookmarked. RhymeZone is the standard. Even if you do not write, browsing rhymes for a random word is one of the better five-minute distractions on the internet.

## What not to bother with

A few categories of "word tools" are not worth bookmarking:

- **Word generators that promise "winning Wordle words"** — they are basically wrong on principle; the best opening word depends on what information you want, not on what is "best"
- **Anagram solvers behind paywalls** — there is no paid anagram solver better than the free ones
- **AI "word coaches"** — they will confidently give you bad advice about word frequency

## Build your own kit

The tools you actually need depend on what you do with words. A writer wants counters and rhymers. A puzzler wants anagram and pattern tools. A language learner wants vocabulary trackers. Pick the two or three that match your habits and skip the rest. A bookmarks folder of 30 word tools you never open is just visual noise.
